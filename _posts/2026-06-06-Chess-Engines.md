**Before he pit me against the computer, my dad wanted to make sure I understood the fundamentals of chess**. I adopted the habit of setting up the pieces when I came home from 2nd grade. Excitement bubbled in my stomach as our match loomed over the dinner table. From his seat at the head of the table, my dad would quiz my sister and I on what we had learned in school that day. It was paramount to answer correctly. A wrong answer meant the rest of the evening would be spent reviewing worksheets. The right answer meant we would get to play chess. When we finally sat down across the board, I could almost imagine my dad and I as equals. I would envision us as war generals facing off in the ultimate battle of wits (even though one general let his son take the bad moves back). We spent months practicing, and the better I played, the more excited he got. A good move received praise, but a great move earned a few seconds of surprised silence. I loved those precious moments when I made my dad think. By summer he was convinced I was ready to play against the computer. 

After our very first match, I hated the computer.

I played against one of the simple Comet engines that came in the Fritz 8 CD, which was a frustrating exercise in futility. All I could do was watch helplessly as each move was punished with frigid precision. I loved playing games with my dad; I loathed playing against the computer. We weren't equals, there were no takebacks, and there were no surprised moments of silence. No matter how good or bad my move was, the precisely calculated response came exactly three seconds later. On nights he worked late, my dad would suggest I continue playing against the engine. It was a dreadful march from the chessboard I set up back to his home office, where the computer monitor glared back at me with my board’s pixellated twin. Throughout each game I felt with absolute certainty that the engine would win, as it had in every game before. So when I finally beat the machine, I didn't feel like celebrating with my dad. Instead, I felt dumbfounded. Victory was not an outcome I considered possible; I was convinced the program had glitched.

**Something like the Comet engine would astonish any chess player from before the late 20th century, who could only encounter an autonomous chess player in the pages of science fiction**. However, a century of hardwork and ingenuity made such a miraculous accomplishment not only possible, but an unremarkable mundanity. Brilliant computer scientists fanned the initial spark of a simple, unthinking automaton with field-defining papers and hardware advancements, burning the fuse towards the earth-shattering showdown: Deep Blue vs. Garry Kasparov. The outcome of that game propelled the conversation outside the orbit of the chess world and into the space of public discourse. Whether deliberately or deservedly, we often use chess prowess as a heuristic for intelligence, and a computer opponent could outwit the strongest human players. 

* Had we finally created a machine that was more intelligent than we are? 
* Was the millennia-spanning game of chess ultimately "solved"? 
* Will we stop playing chess altogether?

These three questions share the same answer.

*No.*

Chess has not declined in popularity. A quick search on platforms such as YouTube or Twitch will result in plenty evidence that chess has found a new generation of fans. Despite this fact, we seem to doubt the validity of such outcomes now that these questions reappear in a new arena. In the field of art, music, and software development, tools such as generative AI bubble up in common conversation as potential “career reapers” poised to similarly outpace human ability. In this article, I break down the history of the chess engine: how the greatest minds in computer science taught a machine to play humanity’s oldest game, and the impact this technology had on those who witnessed its evolution.

## Hiding the Man in the Machine
![Mechanical Turk](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/ff/Racknitz_-_The_Turk_1.jpg)

The first chess playing automaton, named the Mechanical Turk, appeared as though lifted straight from the pages of science fiction. Its inventor, Wolfgang von Kempelen, first demonstrated his creation in Schönbrunn Palace in 1770 to the court of Empress Maria Theresa. The automaton, comprised of a mechanical torso atop an ornate cabinet, stirred commotion in its spellbound audience, comfortably clad in traditional Ottoman garb and lazily smoking a pipe. Von Kempelen would then open the cabinet doors to display the figure’s inner machinery, exposing a dense labyrinth of whirring brass cogs and clockwork levers. He would claim this complex clockwork enabled the Turk to achieve grandmaster-level play, inviting members of the crowd to play a match against the machine. Starting in 1783, the Turk began its career playing across Europe, defeating countless opponents including Napoleon Bonaparte. Decades passed until it finally retired in Philadelphia as a museum exhibition. The touring automaton sparked the imagination of both mathematicians and inventors who tried to reproduce the inner clockwork mechanism. Even in retirement, the Mechanical Turk continued to inspire. The success of the Turk ignited a new era of chess-playing automatons: the devilish Mephisto toured Europe nearly a century after the Turk, and the American automaton Ajeeb astounded audiences on the opposite side of the Atlantic. 

Given its long life in the public eye, it's a miracle Kempelen kept his automaton's secrets long after its retirement. Despite the inventors and skeptics trying to reverse-engineer his creation, Kempelen persisted in maintaining the illusion. The whole truth was not revealed until 1857, three years after the Turk's museum exhibition was consumed in a fire. To create the Mechanical Turk, Kempelen relied on an approach still popular with many AI startup founders today: he lied about how "autonomous" his automaton was.

The Mechanical Turk and the automatons it inspired were entirely incapable of playing chess on their own. Instead, Kempelen found a clever way to hide a man in his machine. Behind the Turk's displayed clockwork mechanism were concealed compartments where a strong chess player could covertly pilot the automaton. Tubes disguised within the Turk's headpiece delivered fresh air to the operator, who would pull levers to watch by candlelight as suspended magnets mirrored their pieces moving over the board. Some eagle-eyed witnesses actually had reported seeing the internal operator, but these testimonies were not believed by the public. Kempelen worked hard to trick the audience, but audiences proved they also wanted to believe such a thing was possible. The future promised by the illusory Mechanical Turk – of a gleaming tomorrow replete with free-thinking, mechanical sentience – was more compelling to discuss than the simple, harsh reality of their time. People did not want to accept the fact that this technology offered no bridge toward the future that existed only in the imagination.  

![El Ajedrecista](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4a/Ajedrecista_segundo1.JPG)

It took a century after the Turk finished its tour before Europe saw its first genuine chess-playing automaton. "El Ajedrecista" (The Chess Player) was invented in 1912 by Spanish engineer Leonardo Torres Quevedo. The automaton simplified the chaos of chess down to a very specific scenario: the King-Rook-King checkmate. Quevedo set up the board so that a human player would move the Black King, and his machine would control the White King and Rook. The chessboard was wired to an electric circuit relay underneath so El Ajedrecista could respond to its opponent’s move. ## Paper Engines

To create automatons that resembled what science fiction authors had envisioned, it was necessary for mathematicians – not authors – to pick up the pen. Engineers continued what Quevedo had started, equipping these machines with more relays and circuitry, but  it was the mathematicians who gave them direction. A few of these prodigious mathematicians were John Von Neumann, Claude Shannon, and Alan Turing, who penned revolutionary papers defining theorems fundamental to chess engines and "paper engines" that raced ahead of their digital contemporaries.

![Minimax Theorem](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1e/Saddle_point.svg)

John Von Neumann's pivotal contribution stems from his seminal 1928 paper, "Zur Theorie der Gesellschaftsspiele"(On the Theory of Board Games). In this work, Von Neumann established the minimax theorem: a crucial component of chess engines that also single-handedly conceived the field of game theory. The theorem states: in any finite, two-player, zero-sum game, one can define a payoff matrix that represents all combinations of moves available to each player. Within this matrix, there exists a pair of optimal strategies for each player, and the value of the game can be calculated. 

When we define these conditions one by one…

* *Finite* - there is an end to the game. 
* *Zero-sum* - anything one player gains is taken from the other player. 
* *Two-player* - I'll give you three guesses as to what this could mean.

…we can see that chess fits the description, and we can define the payoff matrix, too.

* *Payoff matrix* - a table representing the outcomes of the moves taken by two players
  
The minimax theorem allows chess to map onto this payoff matrix: a big grid where each grid square represents a unique board setup. This grid accounts for each possible move combination that two players could ever play. Some of these squares represent the end of a match, or “terminal states”. Because the match ended, the players receive the “payoff” for how they played. Such squares are assigned value based on the outcome of the game:

* *White wins* = `1`
* *Black wins* = `-1`
* *Tie* = `0`

As players make their moves, their game similarly moves through this grid. Each new move is appended to their current move combination, and their move combination acts like navigational directions for traversing the grid. Starting from the initial board setup, following this sequence of moves would lead to the square in the payoff matrix that maps to their game. Both players try to steer the game toward a winning outcome: for White, squares with a value of 1, and for black, squares with a value of  -1. Unlike a human player who can only think a few moves ahead, a theoretical supercomputer could explore every move combination. It could find the best move for each player on their turn, i.e. which move minimizes the number of winning outcomes possible for their opponent and maximizes the number of winning outcomes possible for themselves, until it reaches a terminal state. After finding the optimal strategy for both players, not only could a computer play a game of chess, but the game of chess itself would be solved. Just like El Ajedricista, the computer would know exactly what moves would lead to victory (or at least a tie). Unfortunately for von Neumann and fortunately for chess, even the computers of today, could not consider every possible move sequence in the game. There are more possible chess moves than observable stars in the sky. Von Neumann proved such a winning strategy existed, and it was possible to translate it into something a computer could understand. The practical limitation of the minimax theorem prompted further exploration by computer scientists like John McCarthy, Allen Newell, and notably, Claude Shannon.

Claude Shannon wrote his paper 21 years after von Neumann. After creating the field of information theory, Shannon refined the minimax theorem and laid out two theoretical strategies in his 1949 paper, "Programming a Computer for Playing Chess." The minimax theorem is easy to apply to simple games, but chess is not a simple game. In fact, Shannon estimated there were 10120 possible board configurations in chess, a value subsequently named “Shannon’s number”.  Even today, no computer could sift through the trillions of trillions of possible move sequences to find the absolute optimal strategy. Instead, a chess program would have to strategically consider move sequences to a limited depth, just like human players. He named these strategies Type-A and Type-B: 

* *Type-A Strategy*: a brute-force search that found which moves led to a better outcome
* *Type-B Strategy*: a selective search that discarded moves which led to a worse outcome

However, adding a depth constraint led to a new question: if the game hasn't ended, how does the algorithm decide if the resulting outcome is better or worse? Chess engines would require some way to evaluate the board at any point in the game, or an evaluation function:

* *Evaluation function*: a function that estimates the value of the current state of the game 

The parameters of the evaluation function depend on the game itself and what variables are chosen to estimate the state of the game. Shannon proposed a simple evaluation function based on factors such as counting attacked pieces and opponent-controlled squares, and future programmers would expand their program’s evaluation function further to grant their engine a unique strategy. However, before any computer scientists could code their engines on a computer, Alan Turing decided to try writing a paper engine by hand.

In 1948, Turing outlined his thoughts in "Turochamp", a chess algorithm he hoped to one day run on an actual computer. Turochamp used a Type-A strategy and considered only two moves ahead. Turing also designed his evaluation function to consider pieces remaining, their safety, and their mobility. While it never ran on a computer, Turing tested his "paper" engine by setting up a chessboard and manually calculating the algorithm's next move. He was often disappointed, remarking that the computer would get distracted, "fiddling while Rome burns". The evaluation function was too simple, and the depth of consideration was insufficient. Turochamp didn’t stand a fighting chance against its creator. However, it did earn the honors of being one of the first computer games in history, despite not running on a computer until a decade later. 

These theoretical chess engines may have exceeded the limitations of contemporary hardware, but the computer scientists who optimized these algorithms bridged the gap between chess-playing algorithms and the machines that could run them. It wasn't long before Turochamp, like the Mechanical Turk, was lifted from the page and brought into the physical world.   

## Turning Paper Into Metal

**Once the hardware caught up to the theory written by scientists such as von Neumann, Shannon, and Turing, programmers began exploring the most exciting frontiers of chess engines**. The race began to translate paper engines into executable code. Such algorithms had only begun to outline the fundamental framework, and just like a player progressing to intermediate play, chess engines needed more than the basics to pose a serious threat. Programmers began to arm their chess engines with every tool they had, incorporating openings and endgame tables to guide the engine along lines already mapped by chess theory. Computer scientists further improved such algorithms by finding more ways to imbue the machine with humanlike intuition. All of these contributions would prove necessary to accomplish the final feat standing at the end of the road: victory against the 15-year reigning chess world champion, Garry Kasparov. Three specific achievements that mark that path are Bernstein's Chess Program, Ken Thompson's Belle, and of course, Deep Blue: the engine that marked the end of an era and the birth of a new world.

Alex Bernstein decided to pick up Turing’s fallen baton and run Turochamp on a physical machine. After swapping notes over the phone with Claude Shannon, Bernstein created the first complete chess program in 1957. Aptly named “Bernstein's Chess Program”, he approached his evaluation function by weighing situations in order of decreasing importance, starting with the most important question: "Is my King in check?" The program was a near-identical incarnation of Turochamp, but it adopted Shannon's Type-B strategy to selectively consider "promising" moves. While only capable of amateur play, its existence served greater importance than its capabilities. By creating his program, Bernstein had inadvertently fired a starting pistol. He had proven it was possible to make a computer play chess, but the question remained whether one could write a program to play chess well. Though Bernstein's program was undeniably the first chess program, its reign as the "best" would prove short-lived. It was quickly usurped by the next generation of chess engines, including Ken Thompson's Belle.

While achieving legendary feats such as pioneering the C programming language, the Unix operating system, and the Go programming language, Ken Thompson also set his sights on the popular new trend of chess engine tournaments. Chess engines of his time could not reliably compete against human players, so programmers would pit their programs against each other in tournaments of their own. Thompson first began collaborating with Joe Condon on their contender, Belle, in 1972. Belle notably employed a quiescence search, which tried to "intuit" when a position concealed traps by assessing the “volatility” of the board. Just like human players considering a particular sequence of moves, Belle would variably increase the depth of consideration if its evaluation function predicted hazards lay ahead. Also, Belle benefited from a transposition table, which cached previously seen positions and their resulting evaluation to speed up performance. Thompson's work on Belle earned it both victory and fame. By 1983, Belle became the first chess engine to certifiably play chess at a master level. Belle would also serve as a key ancestor to the most infamous chess engine in history: Deep Blue.

![Deep Blue](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/50/IBM_rs6000_SP_-_Deep_Blue.jpg)

Deep Blue was created by two doctoral students at Carnegie Mellon University, Feng-Hsiung Hsu and Thomas Anantharaman. Though seeming inevitable now, Deep Blue walked a long and arduous road to achieve victory. Deep Blue lost multiple matches against Kasparov in its early stages, and each losing match was accompanied by another round of optimization and improvements. In the end, Hsu and Anatharaman had armed Deep Blue with a veritable arsenal: an evaluation function supported by a database of 700,000 games, opening and endgame tables fine-tuned by Grandmaster Joel Benjamin, and specialized "chess" chips that handled generating, searching, and evaluating moves. In 1997, Deep Blue faced off against Kasparov for the last time. In each of its games, Deep Blue was calculating more than 200 million moves per second. The match ended, and the outcome was clear. A decade of traversing this difficult road had led Hsu and Anatharaman to achieve the impossible: Deep Blue became the first computer to defeat the reigning world champion in a tournament match. Upon defeat, Kasparov accused them of cheating, reminiscent of the opponents who lost to the Mechanical Turk two centuries ago. But Hsu and Anatharaman did not need Kempelen's tricks. **They had successfully taken the man out of the machine.**

## Taking the Man out of the Machine

**Deep Blue defeating Kasparov was an earth-shattering upset, leaving the landscape of the game irrevocably altered**. Deep Blue marked a milestone in the fields of artificial intelligence, computer science, and chess itself. Its victory was soon followed by improvements in both software and hardware, and equally impressive virtual opponents no longer needed a supercomputer to run. The general public could now play against opponents such as the Fritz and Stockfish engines, sparking further discussion about the potential of these chess engines. Having access to such powerful engines only exacerbated public discussion over the implications these programs posed. Just like the audience who witnessed the Mechanical Turk, people would extrapolate what Deep Blue meant for the future of artificial intelligence. Such engines would later be categorized as “classical” chess engines since they functioned similarly to Deep Blue. While the public began to adapt to classical engines, chess algorithms received a new, more alien upgrade that would catapult them far beyond what even the public could imagine.   

Programmers spent the century separating El Ajedrecista and Deep Blue coaching machines to think like humans. The algorithms translated the game of chess into code, and like all translations, the resulting message held the biases of its translator. Quevedo manually defined the steps for checkmate, and Hsu and Anantharaman made Deep Blue consult a gigantic database of grandmaster games. Neural networks discarded such an approach entirely, fully scrubbing those traces of human bias clean from the machine. The most famous of these neural networks, AlphaZero, was developed by the team at Google Deepmind. **If Deep Blue’s victory changed the landscape of the game, then AlphaZero shook the chess world to its core.**

Before AlphaZero, programmers creating chess engines improved on the same core concept: give a computer some opening theory, come up with a function to evaluate midgame moves, and use that function to guide it toward a winnable endgame. But training neural networks allowed programmers to abstract even one step further; it allowed the chess engine to come up with a function on its own. Unlike Deep Blue, able to access hundreds of thousands of games in its database, a neural network starts with a sparse set of information. It only has the basic rules of the game and a representation of the board. Then, it simulates millions of games against itself, virtual pieces moving on the virtual board, ultimately leading to the same three inevitable outcomes: win, loss, or draw. From these exploratory games, the neural network concludes which moves lead to a win or away from defeat and updates its evaluation function. Rather than a programmer dictating the engine to value a strong position or play aggressively, the neural network decides the evaluation function for itself, effectively developing its own playstyle. In 2017, this evolution reached a turning point when AlphaZero faced off against Stockfish 8, the top classical chess engine "champion" at the time. Out of its 100 games, AlphaZero won 28 games, tied 72 games, and lost none. Just as Kasparov had been 20 years prior, the reigning chess champion had been usurped. But while Kasparov and Deep Blue shared similar study materials, AlphaZero was truly self-taught: the only games it analyzed were its own. 

However, the most shocking thing about AlphaZero is not its strength or playstyle. It was in fact not built to play chess. AlphaZero’s architecture was originally built with the intention to master the Chinese strategy board game Go. The Google Deepmind team trained their Go-playing model, AlphaGo Zero, before broadening its application to other strategy games like Chess. The fact that such technology, given only the rules of the games, could teach itself how to outplay classical programs and grandmasters underscored the untapped potential of this technology. Rather than grandmasters tuning the opening theory of a chess program, the dynamic has flipped. Grandmasters like Magnus Carlsen use these neural network engines to hone their own abilities. **We no longer teach chess to the computer. Now, we learn from the computer.**

## Chess Engines
Many feared that once an artificially intelligent player could win against a human, humans would stop playing chess. Such fears have yet to come to fruition. Advancements in chess engines continue to widen the gap between computer and human ability, but that has not diminished the relevance or popularity of chess itself. Instead, players include chess engines as a tool to better analyze games. We continue to play chess because of a simple, unavoidable truth: because it’s fun. 

We also see no matter the century, audiences eagerly buy into the illusion presented to them. The European crowds wanted so earnestly to believe the fantasy promised by the Mechanical Turk that they dismissed eyewitnesses who saw the grandmaster lurking within the automaton. Speculators feared that chess champion Deep Blue marked the sunset of humans playing chess, yet the chess community continues to shine brightly, persevering past the reign of Deep Blue. Centuries have passed and brought new technologies with them, but we still find ourselves daydreaming about the future alongside those European crowds. Unfortunately, modern large-language models pose a greater threat than a nineteenth-century circus attraction, but our reaction to them is largely the same. Time spent imagining a world without work or catastrophizing the exact details of an incoming dystopia is time spent ignoring the fundamental shortcomings of the existing technology. More importantly, it also ignores the harm such technologies already cause to our planet and its people. Time spent in discussion about where a technology will lead prioritizes a future problem that might occur over an immediate problem that is already happening. While these technologies inherit their roots from science fiction, we cannot mistake them for their fictional counterparts. We must contend with our new reality and not entrench ourselves in futuristic delusions.

It is no longer science fiction to imagine that we could face a mechanical mind in chess. We have advanced Quevedo's automaton to a program that even grandmasters now use to study. Computer scientists and chess enthusiasts alike carved a path through a once unknown frontier to escort the computer from a humble heap of circuits to a machine capable of playing chess at a level unrecognizable to its human opponents. Yet we still play chess for the same reason we always have: because it’s fun. It is incredibly difficult for us to master, but that process is what chess players find enjoyable. Future improvements to chess engines will be inducted into how we study the game, not override the desire to play. 

Personally, it doesn't matter to me that I'll never beat the computer, even though I still on occasion rage against the machine. Ultimately, I just want to use it to practice for the next time I get to play against my dad. With enough practice, who knows? Next time I might win.
